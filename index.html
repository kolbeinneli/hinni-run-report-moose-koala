What you're doing brilliantly

Three things to keep doing exactly as you are.

Your consistency is elite-level.

209 runs over 68 weeks — 3.1 runs per week, virtually no gaps. You've run on 44% of all calendar days since January. Volume has climbed from ~20 km/week early in 2025 to ~35 km/week now, with natural build-and-recovery wobble. More consistent than a lot of club athletes.

Your aerobic engine is real.

Your pace at 150–160 bpm dropped 50 s/km (6:29 → 5:39). Efficiency up 11.8% at the same heart rate. And the speed gain comes from longer stride (+5.3%), not cadence — that's the signature of aerobic development, not just "trying harder".

Your long-run base is solid.

22 runs at 90 min or more since January (one every 10 runs), longest 130 min / 21 km. Plenty of endurance substrate for half-marathon and beyond.

Where you're over-invested

Three patterns costing you adaptation without buying much fitness.

The "moderate-hard" black hole.

The biggest single finding: 64% of your running time is at 160 bpm or above (threshold and hard). For building fitness, polarized (80/20) theory says only about 15% should be hard. You're 4× over. Only 10% of your time is truly easy.

Most of your runs live in the tempo zone.

When you DO run easy, you run nicely easy (median 6:08/km at <145 bpm — a healthy 1.24× your 5 k pace). The problem is you don't do it often. Most of your runs sit at 160–175 bpm, where paces are compressed into a narrow 5:30–5:45/km band. A lot of effort, not much aerobic payoff.

Hard days stacked on hard days.

32 of your 133 threshold/hard runs (24%) came with less than 36 hours of recovery before them. That compromises adaptation and quietly accumulates fatigue.

Where you're under-invested

Three zones with the highest return on effort for the next 12 weeks.

Truly easy running.

Only 10% of your time is below 145 bpm. It should be 60–80%. This is the zone that builds mitochondria, capillary density, and fat oxidation — the slow-but-decisive adaptations that make everything else possible.

Cadence.

Median 157 SPM, zero runs at 170 or above. Optimal range is 170–180. Low cadence implies a touch of overstriding (higher impact on your hips and knees) and limits pace efficiency. Your speed gains came entirely from stride length — cadence hasn't moved in 10 months.

True short, sharp intervals.

About 20% of your time is in Z5 (≥175 bpm), but very little of it looks like deliberate repeats with full recovery — more like HR drift or hill surges inside longer runs. Structured intervals (400 m or 1 km reps at 5 k pace, with full jog-back recovery) would raise your fitness ceiling without adding total load.

Your top 5 training tips

In priority order. Doing any one moves the needle. Doing three is transformative.

  1. Use your heart rate to enforce easy, not feel. On about 3 of every 4 runs, cap HR at 145 bpm — walk if you need to. Your target easy pace is 6:45–7:15/km. It will feel ridiculously slow for 2–3 weeks, then you'll start finishing long runs without drift and waking up fresh the next day.
  2. One quality session per week. Real recovery after. Pick one per week: (a) intervals — 8 × 400 m at ~4:50–5:00/km (your 5 k pace), or (b) a tempo — 20 minutes continuous at ~5:15–5:25/km (your 10 k pace). Then give yourself 48 hours minimum before the next hard effort.
  3. Cadence drill — 5 minutes every easy run. Put on a 170-bpm metronome or playlist (there are plenty on Spotify) and run in time with it. Small, fast, light steps at any pace. Over 6–8 weeks your median should drift to 165+, which directly translates to fewer niggles and better pace economy.
  4. Your long run goes longer and easier. Your 90-minute runs are probably averaging 160+ bpm — those are threshold long runs, not aerobic ones. Slow one long run per week down to under 150 bpm and let it drift toward 2 hours over a month or two. Slower pace, more benefit.
  5. Plan a down-week every 4th week. You've been averaging ~35 km/week recently. Drop to ~60% volume (around 21 km) every 4th week. Your volume chart shows natural wobble but no systematic deload — planned dips produce bigger super-compensation peaks afterwards.
